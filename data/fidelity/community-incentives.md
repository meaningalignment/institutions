# Risk-pooled auctions with scored confidence

At community scale — a city, a regional health system, a utility service area — bilateral procurement accumulates the failures of outcome-specified markets, plus the cost of repeated verification overhead and coordination risk across suppliers whose solutions interact.

Community-scale fidelity asks: can the community act as a clearinghouse that pools demand, sockets in common verification, and rewards integrated solutions on terms that price in their structural disadvantages against modular competitors?

Design ingredients in this family of mechanisms:

- Buyers or a pool specify outcome bundles with value, verification criterion, and verification cost.
- Suppliers bid with confidence reports and staked deposits.
- A pool balances solvency over time.
- Transfers compensate integrated suppliers for the resale-option advantage modular competitors enjoy, which otherwise biases the market toward modularity regardless of solution quality.

Design questions:
- At what scale is a socialized verification pool solvent? What empirical conditions govern this?
- How is the pool's governance structured so that it doesn't become captured by repeat-supplier interests?
- What belongs in the bundle-specification stage versus the bidding stage? Who decides bundle structure, and under what constraints?

Adjacent work: health insurance risk pools (for pooling structure); public utility regulation (for multi-supplier coordination); the classic advance-market-commitment literature.
