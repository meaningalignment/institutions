# Lay review panels on the jury-duty model

Citizens called to serve on periodic review panels for institutions in their area — schools, hospitals, government offices, automated decision systems — evaluating not compliance with regulations but whether the institution is living up to its own stated mandate. Modeled roughly on jury duty, with read-access to operational logs and decision traces.

Panels of this kind differ from accreditation (done by credentialed experts against fixed standards) and from inspection (done by regulators against rules): they are ordinary residents examining operations against the institution's own thick mandate. This makes them a civic practice parallel to apprenticeships — the population-level judgment that makes mandate accountability possible.

Design questions:
- How are panelists selected — pure random draw, stratified, with or without opt-out?
- What facilitation do panelists need to engage productively with complex operational data?
- What binding force do panel findings carry? Advisory? Presumptive? Triggering audits or court actions?
- How does the institution under review maintain operational continuity during and after the panel's work?

Adjacent work: citizens' assembly practice in Ireland, France, Canada; participatory budgeting; the German *Schöffen* lay-judge tradition.
