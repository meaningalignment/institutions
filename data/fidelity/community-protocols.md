# Institutional transparency regimes

Institutions already have mandates — a school educates, a platform fosters connection, a hospital heals. What they usually lack is a regime that makes visible (a) what those mandates actually mean in specific terms, (b) what tradeoffs are being made in pursuit of them, and (c) signals from practitioners and affected parties about whether the institution is living up to them.

This transparency is the substrate on which everything else in this column depends. Review panels, audits, courts, and stewardship claims all presuppose that someone can, in principle, examine an institution's mandate against its operations against ground-level reports.

Design questions:
- What granularity of mandate articulation makes the mandate contestable without making it brittle?
- How are practitioner signals — from teachers, caseworkers, nurses — collected without becoming captured by management or by the loudest voices?
- Who has standing to raise a mandate-failure concern, at what evidentiary bar, and at which scale?
- Can one community's transparency regime be audited or federated by another?

Related: freedom-of-information law extended beyond government; accounting-standards bodies (GAAP as a possible analog); longitudinal outcome registries in healthcare; open budgeting practices.
