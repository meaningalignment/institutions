# Apprenticeships that transmit institutional purpose

Medical residencies do not just teach technique — they form judgment about what good care looks like. Legal clerkships do the same for justice. The best apprenticeships in any craft transmit not just skill but an understanding of what the skill is *for*. This is the dyadic engine of practitioner judgment that can recognize when an institution has drifted from purpose.

Design questions:

- What structural features make an apprenticeship transmit *judgment about purpose*, not just skill? How do you tell the transmission is happening?
- Which professions currently have apprenticeship-like structures, and which need them? Accountants, teachers, journalists, and AI developers are plausible candidates with weak or eroding apprenticeship practices.
- How do you protect the unscripted, unspecifiable portion of the apprenticeship — what can only show up in practice — from being squeezed out by competency checklists?
- How do you handle the case where the master has plenty of technical skill but has themselves lost contact with the craft's purpose?

Related reading: Polanyi on tacit knowledge; MacIntyre's *After Virtue* on practice-based goods and their internal standards; Richard Sennett on craft.
