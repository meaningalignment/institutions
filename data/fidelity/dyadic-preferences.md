# Surfacing values, not just positions, in negotiation

**Scenario.** *Edwin and his sister Paulina are negotiating who will take over the family hardware store after their father dies. On the table are ownership percentages, buyouts, who sleeps above the shop. Both of them are bracing for a bad split along preferences. But neither has said out loud what the store was for each of them — for Edwin, a place he could return to when his own business failed; for Paulina, the thing their father built that she watched him love. If they keep negotiating over percentages, they'll settle somewhere, and lose each other. They need a process that surfaces, for each of them, what they actually care about — before the paperwork forecloses it.*

When two parties negotiate — over a contract, a shared project, a collaboration — the standard frame is preference satisfaction. Each has wants; the goal is a settlement both accept. But preferences can be shallow, shaped by what feels feasible, or misaligned with what either party actually values.

A fidelity-oriented frame asks: what does each party want this arrangement to *serve*? Negotiation becomes a process of surfacing what each side finds constitutive, not just their positions.

Design questions:
- How do you elicit values distinct from positions without making the process punishingly slow?
- What role should facilitation — human mediator, LLM, structured protocol — play, and when is each appropriate?
- How do surfaced values stay relevant as circumstances change? Where should a bilateral value-articulation document sit between a contract and a non-binding intention?

Adjacent traditions: interest-based bargaining (Fisher & Ury, *Getting to Yes*); nonviolent communication; deliberative frames in family mediation.
