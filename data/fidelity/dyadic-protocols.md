# Mandate-making practices between parties

Any ongoing pairing — an employment, a care arrangement, a therapeutic or coaching relationship, a long collaboration — rests on some implicit account of what it is *for*. Fidelity at this scale requires making enough of that account explicit to hold either party to it, while leaving room for the relationship to evolve.

Design questions:

- What level of articulation is stable? Too sparse and either party can drift from the shared purpose without noticing; too dense and the relationship becomes a legalistic contract rather than a living practice.
- How are the articulated commitments revised, and by whom, without revision itself becoming a renegotiation whenever one party is dissatisfied?
- How do you protect zones of unarticulated improvisation — the parts of the relationship neither party should have to justify?

Adjacent traditions: relational contracting (Ian Macneil); rules of life in monastic pairings; therapeutic frame-setting; role-defining rituals in long marriages and friendships.
