# Vows grounded in thick mutual commitments

Some dyadic relationships — marriages, adoptions, godparent bonds, lifelong partnerships — historically organized themselves around vows: explicit articulations of what the relationship was for, binding in ways ordinary contracts are not.

Fidelity in these relationships is not preference satisfaction and not performance of discrete obligations. It is sustained enactment of a shared purpose, articulated densely enough that both parties can recognize when one of them has failed it, and committed to in a way that survives ordinary fluctuations in feeling.

Design questions:
- What articulations can modern vows use? Traditional religious vows assumed shared substantive content; a pluralist society needs something that preserves the weight without requiring that specific content.
- How do vows handle revision? What's the relation between "the vow still holds" and "we've both changed"?
- What institutional supports (counselors, witnesses, communities) are needed to keep vows from collapsing into either rigid legalism or empty ceremony?

Adjacent traditions: Catholic sacramental theology on marriage; Confucian relational ethics; secular covenant-marriage experiments; professional vows (the Hippocratic oath as a distant cousin); the practice of spiritual direction.
