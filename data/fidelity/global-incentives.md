# Cross-border pooled procurement

Transnational buyers — treaty organizations, international funders, multinational firms with distributed operations — face the same bundling/modularity asymmetry that domestic outcome-based markets address, amplified by cross-jurisdictional verification costs and currency/legal fragmentation. Cross-border procurement that wants to reward integrated solutions needs mechanisms that neutralize these disadvantages.

Design questions:
- How do transfers that compensate integrated suppliers for modular competitors' resale-option advantage work across jurisdictions with different currencies, cost structures, and demand densities?
- What governance structure for a transnational pool avoids capture by the largest national member or the largest supplier constituency?
- What's the interaction with WTO procurement rules (GPA) and other existing trade-law constraints on procurement?
- How do transnational verification pools handle evaluator independence across political systems?

Adjacent work: the Global Fund and GAVI as partial examples of pooled cross-border procurement; the GPA and its limitations; the literature on sovereign debt and cross-border financial regimes as precedent for transnational institutional design.
