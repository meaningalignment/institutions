# Reputational accountability across borders

When transnational institutions — multinationals, platforms, standards bodies, treaty organizations — drift from their mandates, no single jurisdiction's courts or panels reach them. Some of that gap can be closed by formal cross-border tribunals, but much of it is handled, if at all, by softer cross-border norms: naming practices, reputational mechanisms, coordination among national review panels, expectations carried by professional communities that span borders.

Design questions:
- What's the international equivalent of a review panel or audit? Can national panels share findings credibly when they concern the same multinational?
- How do professional communities that span borders (academic fields, journalistic networks, religious communities) already function as informal accountability-carriers — and what sustains that?
- When norms and formal rules diverge across jurisdictions, which way does coordination have to go? (Cf. the Delaware-effect problem in corporate law.)

Adjacent work: Anne-Marie Slaughter on trans-governmental networks; the role of epistemic communities in international regime formation (Peter Haas); comparative studies of professional standards across borders.
