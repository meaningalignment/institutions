# Transnational markets for complex outcomes

Global commodity markets — agricultural futures, energy, basic goods — succeeded by compressing everything to thin, resellable units. This works for fungible goods, but fails for outcomes where value is in configuration: regional health, biodiversity corridors, coordinated humanitarian response, climate adaptation bundles, transnational public goods.

A transnational market that addresses this would pool demand across jurisdictions, specify outcome bundles, and run auctions with socialized verification. Adjacent work includes pay-for-success contracts, social impact bonds, advance market commitments (the vaccine AMCs and COVAX as partial cases), and outcome-based international transfer pilots.

Design questions:
- How do you measure bundled outcomes — "local food security," "community resilience," "a functioning regional pandemic response" — across radically different political and cultural contexts?
- What counts as a valid evaluator? Who pays for evaluation, and how is independence maintained across jurisdictions?
- How does a cross-border pool handle currency, contract law, and adjudication of failed bundles?
- How does pool governance stay accountable when its members span legal systems with very different norms?

Related work: the development-economics literature on impact evaluation; the procurement literature on public goods at transnational scale; Elinor Ostrom on polycentric governance of transboundary commons.
