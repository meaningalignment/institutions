# Deliberative aggregation of global preferences

**Scenario.** *A frontier-AI safety body is about to issue binding deployment guidance that will shape how the next generation of models is released worldwide. The technical analysis is in hand; what's missing is any defensible answer to "on what authority?" Expert consensus has it, state positions filtered through diplomatic machinery have it, polling-shaped public opinion has it — but none of these is "what informed humanity, having actually considered the tradeoffs, converged on." The chair, Adaeze, wants a process whose output the body can point to as the public mandate, and that the frontier labs can be held to over time as the mandate moves. She doesn't need it to be binding. She needs it to be legitimating.*

Global commodity markets compressed value to fungible units; recommender systems compressed preference to engagement signal. Both succeeded at scale by giving up on substance. What's missing at the global tier is an institution that aggregates *considered* preference — what people would say after engaging with the question, not what they click — and produces a moving mandate that downstream institutions can be held accountable to.

A deliberative aggregation institution would run structured, AI-assisted deliberation across linguistic and cultural lines on a small number of planetary-stakes questions per year, and produce a public output: not "65% favor X" but "after engaging with the tradeoffs, participants converged on these positions, diverged on these, and named these as the considerations any decision must address." Adjacent work includes Citizens' Assemblies (Ireland, France, the UK climate assembly), Taiwan's vTaiwan, Pol.is at smaller scales, the Global Assembly on the Climate and Ecological Crisis, and deliberative-polling pilots by Fishkin and others. The AGI angle is double-edged: agents are the thing that makes planetary deliberation logistically tractable (translation, summarization, agenda-setting, helping individuals form considered views), *and* they're the thing that makes the existing fallbacks (polls, social media, revealed preference) untrustworthy enough at scale that you actually need this.

Design questions:
- How is the agenda set — what gets deliberated each year, and who decides? Self-selecting topics get captured; centrally chosen topics inherit the chooser's legitimacy problem.
- How are participants selected and how is participation made real across jurisdictions where attending a multi-day process is impossible? Stratified sampling plus AI-assisted async deliberation, or something else?
- What's the role of agents inside the deliberation — translation, summarization, devil's-advocacy, helping participants form views, or all of these? How is agent influence on the output bounded and made auditable?
- What does the output look like such that a treaty body, a frontier lab, or a national government can be held accountable to it? A set of converged positions, a list of constraints the mandate names, a public scorecard against which downstream institutions are evaluated?
- How does the institution stay uncaptured — by states, by platforms, by the firms whose behavior the mandate is meant to constrain? Whose funding, whose governance, whose audit?

Related work: Habermas on the public sphere and discourse ethics; the deliberative democracy literature (Fishkin, Chambers, Landemore); Citizens' Assembly practice; Pol.is and vTaiwan; the Global Assembly experiments; recent proposals for a UN Parliamentary Assembly and global citizens' panels.
