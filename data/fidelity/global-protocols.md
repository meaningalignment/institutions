# Translating institutional mandates across jurisdictions

Fidelity presupposes that some articulations of what an institution is for engage more deeply with purpose than others, and that this depth is recognizable across worldviews often enough to ground accountability. At global scale, this becomes a translation question: what value structures travel, what stays local, and how are they related?

Broad legibility is one of the conditions for fidelity to function as a public value. A Catholic natural-law tradition and a secular Rawlsian should both recognize when a school has lost its educational mission, even if they disagree on what schooling is ultimately for.

Design questions:
- Can substantive value articulations be made in forms portable across cultural and religious traditions, or do they need tradition-specific expression?
- What coordination do cross-border enterprises (platforms, pharmaceutical firms, cross-national services) need when their mandates implicate multiple legal and moral traditions?
- How do global protocols avoid becoming a vehicle for technocratic or ideological homogenization?
- Which existing international standards (ISO, accounting, medical) offer useful analogies, and where do they fall short for substantive value content?

Related: Charles Taylor, *Modern Social Imaginaries*; comparative constitutional law on translation of rights concepts across jurisdictions.
