# International tribunals for institutional accountability

**Scenario.** *A large platform, incorporated in one country, operating primarily from a second, with most of its users in a third, has for years run a help-each-other-with-work feature that its founding materials committed to "restoring dignity to day labor." Over the last three years, the platform has quietly shifted the feature into a piecework marketplace with a take-rate its own founding documents would have flagged. Complaints to each jurisdiction's regulators have been passed around and dropped; no one court has reach, and no one government wants to go first. A group of workers' cooperatives from six countries wants to bring a single claim — in a forum none of them has access to. They need one to exist.*

Some institutional failures are transnational by their nature: a multinational platform's drift from its stated mandate affects users across jurisdictions, none of which individually has sufficient leverage; a pharmaceutical firm's patent portfolio serves no innovation mandate in any single country's law. A cross-border adjudication forum would hear claims that transnational institutions have drifted from the mandates under which they hold public commitments.

Design questions:
- What's the forum's jurisdiction? Consent-based (like ICSID investment tribunals)? Sectoral (for specific industries)? Triggered by national panel findings?
- What remedies can it order that national courts cannot? Stewardship reassignment of trademarks, patents, or datasets across borders is a concrete candidate.
- How do limiting doctrines — in rem, ripeness, protected zones — translate to the international setting?
- How is the forum kept independent of both geopolitical blocs and the defendants it hears?

Adjacent work: ICSID and investment-treaty arbitration (as a cautionary tale about capture); the WTO dispute-settlement body; the ICJ and ICC (as institutional reference points); the emerging business-and-human-rights regime (UNGPs, the draft treaty).
