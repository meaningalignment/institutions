# Traditions of institutional purpose across worldviews

For mandate-accountability to function as a global public value, it must be broadly legible: across different worldviews, people must be able to recognize the value within their own experience and use it in their own arguments. Freedom and fairness did this in their ways.

At global scale, this means articulating what institutional accountability asks of organizations in forms that a Confucian, a Rawlsian, a natural-law Catholic, and a secular humanist can each recognize as engaging with what their own tradition takes education, governance, or craft to be for. Not a common substantive ethic — a shared grammar of institutional accountability that each tradition fills in from its own resources.

Design questions:
- Which translations already exist in existing traditions? (Confucian *zhong* — loyalty-to-role; Christian fiduciary tradition; Islamic waqf governance; Indigenous stewardship traditions.)
- What institutional forms (panels, courts, charters, vows) travel across traditions vs. require tradition-specific expression?
- How do transnational institutions maintain accountability across the traditions their members belong to, without flattening any?

Related reading: Charles Taylor on modern social imaginaries and *A Secular Age*; Alasdair MacIntyre on tradition and practice; the comparative political-theory literature (Andrew March, Farah Godrej).
