# Hands-on rotation within teams

Many practice-based groups — newsrooms, consulting firms, architecture practices, teaching faculties, small hospitals — depend on a core of senior practitioners whose judgment keeps the group's work tethered to its mandate. When that core erodes — through automation, specialization, or generational turnover — the group can continue operating while silently losing the ability to recognize when it has drifted.

Design questions:
- What's actually being maintained — skill, judgment, taste, institutional memory? Each has different structural requirements.
- What mix of rotation, sabbaticals, hands-on minimums, and mentorship preserves the base at acceptable cost?
- How does the group know the maintenance is working? What signals that practitioners can still recognize drift?
- How do you handle domains where automation is now genuinely better at the legible tasks, so that rotation imposes quality costs on each specific case?

Adjacent work: Polanyi on tacit knowledge; clinical training literature on autonomy vs. supervision; Christensen on the innovator's dilemma as a special case.

## Problem Sets

### Domain Practitioners as Mandate Witnesses

A professional association (choose one: lawyers, doctors, teachers, architects, software engineers, journalists) wants to formalize a practice: at regular intervals, senior practitioners convene to assess whether the profession is still doing what it's for. Not disciplinary boards evaluating individual cases; not regulatory compliance reviews. A thicker question about the craft's mandate in the current environment — where has the profession drifted, where has it deepened, what new conditions require rearticulation of what it's for?

**The problem:** Design this practice.

1. **Who convenes.** Not just elites, not just dissenters, not just volunteers. How do you constitute the body without creating a new elite or a self-selected grievance caucus?
2. **What they examine.** Not individual cases, not just outcome metrics. What's the unit of analysis — patterns across cases, shifts in training pipelines, changes in how the profession is talked about, something else?
3. **Output.** A report? A moral graph? A set of flagged drifts with examples? What form makes the finding usable, and by whom?
4. **Teeth.** Does a finding trigger anything formal — an audit, a licensing review, a charter revision? Or is it purely a clarifying ritual whose only power is public visibility?
5. **Failure modes.** How might the practice itself drift? What does its capture or irrelevance look like, and what structural features resist that?

**Deliverable:** The charter for one profession's mandate-witness practice, plus an honest account of which drifts the practice would catch and which it would miss.
