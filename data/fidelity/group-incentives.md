# Internal rewards that track mandate

Inside a group — team, small firm, project — compensation and recognition usually attach to inputs (hours worked, deliverables shipped) or thin outputs (revenue, engagement, closed tickets). These often diverge from what the group is actually for, producing the internal equivalent of institutional drift.

The design problem: structure internal rewards (bounties, profit shares, promotions, recognition) so they track outcomes thick enough to represent the group's mandate, without collapsing back into Goodhart-prone metrics.

Design questions:
- How do you specify internal outcome bundles rich enough to resist gaming?
- What's the role of peer-rated confidence staking vs. manager allocation vs. automated scoring?
- How do long-horizon outcomes get rewarded without punishing people whose good decisions haven't paid off yet?
- How do you avoid creating internal incentive schemes so elaborate they crowd out intrinsic motivation?

Adjacent work: Ostrom on internal governance of commons (*Governing the Commons*); classical multitask principal-agent theory (Holmstrom & Milgrom); the "measuring what matters" literature on OKRs, all of which struggles with the thickness problem.
