# Informal practices that keep purpose alive

Beyond charter documents and formal bylaws, groups sustain (or fail to sustain) fidelity through informal practices: the rituals, conversations, and shared habits that keep the question of purpose alive. This is fidelity in the register of norms rather than rules.

Examples of what these practices might look like:

- Periodic "what are we doing this for" retreats that produce no binding output but recenter the group.
- Exit interviews that ask departing members to name drifts they saw.
- Standing slots in meetings for raising purpose-level concerns, separate from operational business.
- Mentorship or buddy arrangements across seniority levels, so that newer members carry perspective senior members have lost.

Design questions:
- How do groups institutionalize these practices without reducing them to compliance theater?
- What triggers a genuine mandate-review vs. ordinary grievance venting?
- How do these norms travel when the group scales, merges, or turns over?

Adjacent traditions: Quaker meetings for business; the Jesuit examen; agile retrospectives done honestly; board-level mission review. The common feature is a container in which the purpose question can be raised without being coded as disloyalty.
