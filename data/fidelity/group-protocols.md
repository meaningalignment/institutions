# Charters specifying mandate, tradeoffs, and revision procedure

A group — team, club, collective, small firm — can operate for years on an implicit sense of what it's doing. Fidelity at group scale requires turning enough of that into shared, legible articulation that members can call the group back to its purpose when it drifts.

What needs to be articulable:

1. **Mandate.** What the group is for, in terms rich enough to distinguish smart compromises from betrayals. Too thin a mandate ("foster connection") equivocates; too rigid a mandate ossifies.
2. **Tradeoffs.** Where the group will trade off its primary purpose against secondary concerns like growth, efficiency, or cohesion — and where it refuses to.
3. **Revision procedure.** How the mandate itself can be deepened or revised as the group learns, without becoming a vehicle for whichever demands currently dominate the room.
4. **Protected zones.** The parts of the group's work deliberately left unarticulated — craft judgment, improvisation, unstructured time.

Design questions:
- What's the minimum articulation a group needs to remain accountable to itself? What's the maximum before it becomes brittle?
- How do you write a revision procedure that admits learning but resists capture?
- What are good exemplars of group-scale mandate documents? (Cooperative bylaws, religious community rules, small-firm partnership agreements.)
