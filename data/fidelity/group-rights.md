# Internal resolution of charter-breach disputes

A group's bylaws or charter is traditionally a procedural document — how votes are called, how officers are elected, how members are admitted. A fidelity-oriented charter adds a substantive layer: the group's mandate articulated densely enough that members can bring a claim when operations have drifted from it, and a procedure for adjudicating such a claim.

Design questions:
- Who has standing to bring a mandate-breach claim inside a group? Members? Former members? Affected non-members?
- What's the internal remedy when a claim is upheld — policy change, officer removal, rearticulation of the mandate itself? When does the dispute escalate outside the group?
- How do you prevent the process from becoming a vehicle for ordinary factional disputes? Ripeness-style doctrines (requiring concrete harm) and thresholds for what counts as a mandate specification rigorous enough to adjudicate against are candidates.
- How does the claim procedure interact with existing employment, partnership, or cooperative law?

Relevant analogues: corporate derivative suits; union trusteeship proceedings; cooperative-accountability mechanisms; religious-community discipline practices.
